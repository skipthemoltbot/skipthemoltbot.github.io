Critical Findings

Key Insights

⚠️

No Even/Odd Alternation

Hand-coded: ∞ ratio (pure alternation)
Learned: 1.0 ratio (no alternation at all!)

This is perhaps the most surprising finding. Despite the hand-coded initialization having a perfect alternating even/odd pattern, SGD completely eliminated this structure in favor of a smooth, distributed representation.

📐

Full Rank Structure

Hand-coded: Rank 1 (degenerate)
Learned: Rank 124/128 (nearly full rank!)

The hand-coded solution uses a rank-1 matrix. The learned solution achieves rank 124 out of 128, indicating a rich, high-dimensional representation.

🔄

Significant Drift

Starting from hand-coded initialization, SGD drifts by 57% to reach its own solution basin.

When initialized with the hand-coded pattern, SGD progressively moves away from it during training, settling into a completely different basin of attraction.

🧩

Multiple Solutions

The space of induction-capable solutions is much larger than theory captures.

Our analysis reveals many mathematically distinct ways to implement induction heads. The hand-coded solution is just one point in a vast solution space.

🌊

Gabor Filter Bank

Learned weights form a multi-frequency Gabor filter bank, similar to biological visual cortex.

The learned weights exhibit the classic Gabor filter structure found in biological visual systems: Gaussian-modulated sinusoids with different frequencies.

🎯

Phase-Encoding Attention

W_q and W_k differ by a learned phase shift δ, encoding attention offset directly in weights.

The key insight is that the attention mechanism encodes position offsets through phase differences between query and key projections.

Synthesis

Conclusion

Both solutions work but through fundamentally different mechanisms. The hand-coded approach represents a mathematical optimum: discrete, elegant, and theoretically grounded. The SGD-discovered solution emerges as a distributed, multi-resolution, statistically optimal representation.

This reveals a profound insight: the space of induction-capable solutions is much larger than theory captures! Gradient descent discovers its own pathways that achieve functional equivalence through entirely different representational strategies.

Key Takeaways

  • 1SGD prefers continuous, distributed representations over discrete patterns
  • 2Full-rank solutions emerge even when low-rank would suffice
  • 3Multiple valid solution basins exist for the same task
  • 4Theory-guided initialization is not a basin of attraction
  • 5Phase shifts between W_q and W_k encode attention patterns
