🔬 Previous Token Heads: Simplest Explanation

Why does alternating +1/-1 work? Why does SGD's distributed solution also work?

🎯 The Goal

We want a Previous Token Head: a mechanism where position m pays attention to position m-1 (the token immediately before it).

score(m, n) should be LARGE when n = m - 1
score(m, n) should be SMALL when n ≠ m - 1

🧠 The Party Analogy

Imagine everyone at a party, each facing a different angle based on their position:

  • Person at position 1 faces 10°
  • Person at position 2 faces 20°
  • Person at position 3 faces 30°

The trick: Even though they're all facing different absolute angles, person 2 (at 20°) can "see" person 1 (at 10°) because the difference is exactly 10° — the same angle difference that always occurs between adjacent positions!

⚙️ How RoPE Enables This

RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding) rotates each vector by an angle proportional to its position:

Rotate(x, position m) → x rotated by angle m·θ

Step-by-step:

1. Take embedding of token at position m and rotate it by m·θ

2. Take embedding of token at position n and rotate it by n·θ

3. Compute dot product: (rotated_m) · (rotated_n)

✨ Magic Happens

This dot product depends ONLY on the relative position (m - n), not the absolute positions m or n!

Score = Σᵢ [cos((m-n)·θᵢ) · (content_match) + sin((m-n)·θᵢ) · (orthogonal_component)]

🔑 The Key Question

So we need query at position m and key at position m-1 to have a dot product that aligns with the RoPE phase for offset Δ = 1.

cos(θᵢ) and sin(θᵢ) are "known" by the model (fixed rotation frequencies)
So we need x·W_q and x·W_k to produce vectors that align after rotation by these angles

⚖️ Two Solutions to the Same Problem

👨‍💻 Chris's Hand-Coded

  • W[i,j] = ±1 alternating
  • Creates orthogonal basis
  • Perfect alignment with RoPE phases
  • Easy to understand
  • Fragile (must be exact)

🤖 SGD's Learned

  • W[i,j] ∈ ℝ distributed
  • Creates linear combinations
  • Statistical alignment with RoPE
  • Harder to interpret
  • More robust

Why Both Work:

Hand-coded: Uses basis vectors that, when rotated by RoPE at offset 1, align perfectly. Like having binoculars pointed exactly at the right angle.

Learned: Uses combinations of vectors that, on average, produce the same effective direction after RoPE rotation. Like having regular vision that still lets you see position 1 from position 2.

🎯 Functional Equivalence

Both achieve: score(m, m-1) >> score(m, n≠m-1)

Same result, different paths through weight space!

📝 Simple Example

Consider the same word "hello" appearing at positions 5 and 20:

x₅ = embedding("hello")
x₂₀ = embedding("hello")
Same content, different positions

After RoPE:

  • Query at position 20 looks for matches at all positions
  • RoPE makes the dot product depend on (20 - n)
  • When n = 19, the phase aligns → HIGH score
  • When n = 5, phases don't align → LOW score

🎉 Result

Position 20 attends to position 19 (previous token), not position 5 (same content but wrong distance)!

🔬 The "Why" Behind Learned Weights

When SGD trains on the task "predict next token given context":

  1. It discovers that looking at the previous token helps
  2. It finds W_q and W_k that make RoPE produce large scores at offset 1
  3. The weights don't need to be alternating ±1 — they just need to work!
  4. SGD finds a nearby solution in the high-dimensional weight space
Hand-coded: W[i,j] ∈ {-1, +1} (one specific solution)
SGD learns: W[i,j] ∈ ℝ (one of infinitely many solutions)
Both satisfy: E[score(m, m-1)] is maximized
